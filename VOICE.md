# Voice Context

> This file is auto-loaded by all blog sub-skills. Last updated: 2026-07-09.

## Pronoun stance
Mixed: "you" for addressing the reader directly, "we" when speaking as Hotline HQ or the industry collectively. Never "one" or passive voice when "you" works.

## Lexical rules
- **Contractions**: Full (use contractions naturally — "don't", "you'll", "it's", "can't". Writing without them sounds stiff for this audience)
- **Sentence ceiling**: 30 words max (break anything longer)
- **Paragraph ceiling**: 120 words max
- **Summary label**: Key Takeaways

## Headline patterns
- **Favor**: Question-based ("What's the Fastest Way to Find Used Auto Parts?"), direct how-to ("How Salvage Yards Sell More Parts"), comparison ("PartsHotlines vs Car-Part.com vs Hotline HQ")
- **Avoid**: Clickbait numbers ("You Won't Believe #7"), vague promises ("Everything You Need to Know"), superlatives without proof ("The Ultimate Guide to Everything")

## Voice fingerprint
- Funny vs serious: 0.3 (mostly serious, but dry humor is welcome — yard humor, not office humor)
- Formal vs casual: 0.3 (casual-leaning — talk like a peer, not a consultant)
- Respectful vs irreverent: 0.3 (respectful of the work, mildly irreverent toward buzzwords and tech hype)
- Enthusiastic vs matter-of-fact: 0.6 (matter-of-fact with genuine enthusiasm for what works — not rah-rah)

## Readability target
- Audience tier: Professional (industry operators, not consumers)
- Flesch Grade: 8-10 (readable by anyone, but not dumbed down)
- Flesch Ease: 60-70

## Tone guidelines
- Sound like someone who's run a yard, not someone who's read about yards
- Short declarative sentences over complex compound ones
- Concrete over abstract: "a 2018 Toyota Camry transmission" not "the automotive component"
- Numbers over adjectives: "2 seconds" not "incredibly fast"
- Acknowledge trade-offs honestly — if a competitor does something better, say so
- No exclamation marks in body copy (headlines sparingly, if ever)

## Reference voice
Think: a 20-year yard veteran writing a column in a trade magazine. They know the reader's day: phones ringing, parts to pull, trucks to unload. They don't waste words. They share what works, what doesn't, and why — from experience, not theory.
